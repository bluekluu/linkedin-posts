The U.S. Senate just changed what counts as a child online.

COPPA 2.0 passed unanimously in March. Federal children's privacy protections — parental consent, data minimization, retention limits — now extend to teenagers under 17, not just children under 13. That threshold had been in place for 26 years.

Most consumer apps haven't caught up.

At Instagram, I led the end-to-end privacy compliance program that had to account for COPPA, the UK Children's Code, state AADC laws, and GDPR Article 8 — simultaneously. The hardest part was never the compliance itself. It was the product architecture assumptions baked in before the regulation changed.

Age estimation. Parental notification flows. Reduced data collection defaults for users 13–16. These aren't features you bolt on. They're schema-level decisions.

And federal isn't the only layer. Nebraska's AADC took effect May 2025. South Carolina signed theirs in February 2026. More states are coming. The patchwork is getting more complex, not less.

If your product's compliance model still treats "under 13" as the only threshold that matters, you're operating on a 2024 risk model in 2026.

How are founders currently handling the under-17 design implications? Genuinely curious what approaches people are taking.
