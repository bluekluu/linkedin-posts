"We don't target kids" is no longer a compliance defense.

The Senate passed COPPA 2.0 unanimously in March. The new standard isn't whether you knew minors were on your platform — it's whether you should have known, based on objective circumstances.

That shift matters more than most product teams realize.

At Instagram, kids-on-platform was one of the most technically and legally complex problems we navigated — not because the law was unclear, but because the signals were everywhere and the incentives to look away were strong. Age verification is hard. Inference from behavioral signals is legally fraught. Parental consent flows kill conversion.

Here's what founders often underestimate: COPPA 2.0 doesn't just raise the age threshold from 13 to 17. It creates a right to deletion for minors. It strengthens parental consent requirements. And it expands what counts as personal information.

If your user base skews young, or if your product could reasonably attract teenagers, audit your data flows now — not after the House passes the bill.

This is the compliance work that takes 6–9 months to do properly. The clock is already running.
